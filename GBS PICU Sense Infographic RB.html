Overview
Epidemiology
Incidence: 0.5–2 / 100,000 children/year; bimodal peak age 4–9y & adolescence
Sex ratio: M > F (1.5:1)
Preceding infection: 60–70% — symptoms 1–4 weeks post illness
Most common cause of acute flaccid paralysis in children post-polio era
Mortality: 3–5% (higher with respiratory failure, autonomic instability)
Triggers: C. jejuni, CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma, COVID-19, Zika, Flu vaccine (1:1M)
⚡ ICU Admission Criteria
Brighton Criteria Level ≥3 or bilateral weakness + areflexia
FVC <20 mL/kg or rapidly declining
20-30-40 Rule: FVC <20 OR MIP <30 cmH₂O OR MEP <40 cmH₂O → intubate
Bulbar dysfunction: dysphagia, dysarthria, aspiration risk
Autonomic instability: labile HR/BP, arrhythmias
Hughes score ≥4 (bedbound / ventilated)
Rapid progression: <5 days from onset to peak weakness
Pathogenesis
01
Trigger Infection
C. jejuni, CMV, EBV, SARS-CoV-2 — lipooligosaccharides mimic gangliosides (GM1, GD1a, GQ1b) on peripheral nerves → molecular mimicry
02
Antibody Production
Anti-ganglioside IgG/IgM formed (anti-GM1, GD1a, GQ1b, GD1b) targeting gangliosides on peripheral nerve axons/myelin
03
Complement Activation
Antibody binding → complement cascade → MAC deposition → Schwann cell / axonal membrane injury
04
T-Cell Infiltration
T-lymphocytes & macrophages cross blood-nerve barrier → macrophage-mediated myelin stripping or direct axonal attack
05
Nerve Dysfunction
Demyelination → slowed conduction, conduction blocks. Axonal injury → Wallerian degeneration → denervation, prolonged recovery
Subtypes & Variants

AIDP
Acute Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy
Most common in west (~85%)
Demyelinating → slow NCV, prolonged F-waves, conduction blocks
Variable Ab Demyelinating EMG
AMAN
Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy
Common in Asia & children; assoc. C. jejuni
Pure motor; faster or more severe recovery
Anti-GM1, GD1a IgG Axonal motor EMG
AMSAN
Acute Motor & Sensory Axonal Neuropathy
Severe; motor + sensory axonal loss
Slow/incomplete recovery; high disability
Anti-GM1, GD1a IgG Poor prognosis
MFS
Miller Fisher Syndrome
Triad: ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia
May overlap AIDP; Bickerstaff if encephalopathy
Anti-GQ1b ≥85% Good prognosis
BBE / PCB
Bickerstaff Brainstem Encephalitis / Pharyngeal-Cervical-Brachial
BBE: ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + encephalopathy
PCB: oropharyngeal + neck + arm weakness
Anti-GQ1b, GT1a Bulbar risk!
Clinical Features
Motor Features
Ascending symmetric flaccid weakness: legs → arms → bulbar → respiratory
Areflexia (or hyporeflexia) — hallmark finding, early and universal
CN VII: bilateral facial palsy common; CN IX/X: bulbar involvement
Respiratory muscle failure: diaphragm + intercostals → type II respiratory failure
Children: inability to walk, pain in legs, refusal to bear weight
Peak weakness: typically by day 14–28; plateau phase follows
Sensory & Pain
Paresthesias: glove-and-stocking; often first symptom
Neuropathic pain: back/limb pain — children often present with pain before weakness
Vibration and proprioception loss (large fiber dysfunction)
AMAN: sensory preserved despite severe motor involvement
Allodynia / hyperalgesia in acute phase
⚠ Autonomic Features
Sinus tachycardia (most common) or bradycardia
Labile BP: hypertension alternating with hypotension
Cardiac arrhythmias: AV block, ventricular ectopy → continuous cardiac monitoring mandatory
Ileus, urinary retention, constipation, SIADH
Dysautonomia in 50–70% hospitalized GBS; 25% of children
Avoid suctioning without premedication — vagal surges → cardiac arrest risk

Neurological Exam
Motor & Reflex
AREFLEXIA / HYPOREFLEXIA
DTRs absent/markedly reduced globally; most sensitive early sign. In AMAN reflexes may be present early — reassess serially.
FLACCID WEAKNESS
Hypotonia, no spasticity, no clonus. Proximal ≥ distal. Symmetric. Hip flexion often early.
CRANIAL NERVE PALSY
Bilateral CN VII palsy ~50%; dysphagia/dysphonia (CN IX/X); extraocular palsy (MFS). Absent gag → aspiration risk.
ABSENT: UMN SIGNS
No Babinski, no spasticity, no hyperreflexia → distinguishes from myelopathy.
Sensory & Special Tests
SENSORY LOSS (AIDP/AMSAN)
Vibration ↓↓ (125Hz tuning fork), proprioception ↓↓. Glove-and-stocking. Light touch/pinprick variably reduced.
ROMBERG SIGN
Positive (falls with eyes closed) when proprioceptive loss prominent.
OPHTHALMOPLEGIA / PTOSIS
Full/partial; all directions (MFS). Pupillary reflex usually spared. Diplopia = early MFS.
RESPIRATORY ASSESSMENT
Paradoxical breathing, count-to-20 single breath, bedside FVC. Neck flexor weakness predicts ventilator need.
Diagnostic Workup
Brighton Criteria (Levels 1–4)
LEVEL 1 — Highest certainty
Bilateral flaccid weakness + DTR ↓/absent + monophasic + CSF cytoalbuminous dissociation + EMG peripheral neuropathy
LEVEL 2
Bilateral flaccid weakness + DTR ↓/absent + monophasic + (CSF OR EMG criteria — not both required)
LEVEL 3
Bilateral flaccid weakness + DTR ↓/absent + monophasic course only (no CSF/EMG available)
LEVEL 4 — Unclassified
Suspected GBS but does not meet levels 1–3, or alternative diagnosis confirmed

Treatment
🚫
Corticosteroids — CONTRAINDICATED
Steroids do NOT improve GBS outcomes and may worsen prognosis. Multiple RCTs negative. Do NOT give dexamethasone or methylprednisolone.
⚠
Treatment Window
IVIg and PLEX effective only within 2–4 weeks of onset. Combination not superior to either alone. Choose one modality.
Disease-Modifying Therapy
IVIg — First Line
Preferred in children (easier administration than PLEX)
Dose: 1 g/kg/day × 2 days OR 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days (equivalent)
Mechanism: Fc receptor blockade, complement inhibition, anti-idiotype Abs
CI: IgA deficiency (anaphylaxis — check IgA first!), severe heart failure
SE: headache, aseptic meningitis, thromboembolic events, hemolysis, renal failure (sucrose-based)
Monitor: IgA, renal function, CBC
PLEX — Equivalent
Equivalent to IVIg; preferred if IVIg contraindicated or failed
Protocol: 4–6 sessions over 8–10 days; 200–250 mL/kg total removed
Replace with albumin (FFP if coagulopathy)
Challenges: vascular access, hemodynamic instability in young children
CI: active sepsis, severe coagulopathy, hemodynamic instability
Re-treatment
Treatment-related fluctuation (TRF): 5–10% worsen after initial improvement → repeat IVIg
TRF occurs within 8 weeks; CIDP if >8–12 weeks. SID-GBS trial: second IVIg dose in high-IGOS patients.
ICU Supportive Care
Airway
Early planned intubation preferred (20-30-40 Rule)
RSI: avoid succinylcholine (hyperkalemia risk) → use rocuronium
NIV caution in bulbar dysfunction; tracheostomy if >2 weeks ventilation expected
Autonomic
Continuous cardiac monitoring mandatory
Labile BP: short-acting agents only; avoid long-acting antihypertensives
Bradycardia: transcutaneous pacing available; atropine for acute severe bradycardia
Avoid beta-blockers + anticholinergics
Pain & DVT
Neuropathic pain: gabapentin 10–35 mg/kg/day ÷ TID or pregabalin (adolescents)
DVT: LMWH (enoxaparin) once ambulation lost + sequential compression devices
Refractory pain: consider IV ketamine infusion
Rehab & Nursing
Early physio/OT; passive ROM from day 1; pressure area care
Nutritional support: dietitian, NGT if dysphagia
Psychological support: communication aids for conscious ventilated patients

Poor Prognostic Factors
  • ⬤ Rapid progression to peak weakness (<7 days)
  • ⬤ Preceding C. jejuni infection (AMAN phenotype)
  • ⬤ Axonal subtype (AMAN/AMSAN) on EMG
  • ⬤ Mechanical ventilation required at presentation
  • ⬤ Older age (younger children have better recovery)
  • ⬤ Absent SNAPs and absent CMAPs on EMG
  • ⬤ Hughes score ≥5 at nadir
  • ⬤ Low IVIg serum level post-treatment
  • ✓ Good factors: MFS subtype, young age, slow progression, sensory > motor
IGOS Score
Predicts inability to walk at 6 months. Factors: age, preceding diarrhea, GBS disability score at admission, mean distal CMAP amplitude. Score ≥6 predicts ventilatory support need. Used to guide treatment aggressiveness and ICU resource planning.
PICU Clinical Pearls
Ventilator Management
Lung-protective: TV 6–8 mL/kg, PEEP 5–8 cmH₂O
Preserve spontaneous breathing if possible (CPAP/SIMV)
Avoid NMBA — masks neurological assessment
Weaning: SBT only when FVC trending upward ×48h
Most children extubate within 4–8 weeks
Autonomic Crisis Protocol
Severe HTN: labetalol IV (avoid long-acting agents)
Hypotension: IV fluids first; vasopressors prn
Bradycardia: transcutaneous pacing on standby
NO vigorous suctioning without atropine premedication
Avoid β-blockers; careful opioid dosing (↑ vagal tone)
Daily Monitoring Checklist
✓ FVC q4–6h (more frequent if declining)
✓ Continuous cardiac telemetry
✓ Daily neurological exam (Hughes score)
✓ Neuropathic pain assessment (FLACC in young children)
✓ Nutritional assessment daily
✓ Pressure area inspection q shift
✓ DVT prophylaxis review
✓ Psychosocial needs: family + patient
