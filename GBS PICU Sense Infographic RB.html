GUILLAIN-BARRÉ
SYNDROME

Comprehensive ICU Reference · Children & Adolescents · GBS & Variants
⚠ Respiratory Emergency Risk Neuromuscular · Autoimmune Evidence-Based · 2024
⬡ Epidemiology & Overview
Incidence: 0.5–2 / 100,000 children/year; bimodal peak age 4–9y & adolescence
Sex ratio: M > F (1.5:1); slight male predominance
Preceding infection: 60–70% — symptoms begin 1–4 weeks after respiratory/GI illness
Most common cause of acute flaccid paralysis in children in the post-polio era
Mortality: 3–5% (higher with respiratory failure, autonomic instability, ICU admission)
Common triggers in children: C. jejuni (→ AMAN), CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma, COVID-19, Zika, Flu vaccine (1:1M)
⚡ ICU Admission Indications
Brighton Criteria Level ≥3 or bilateral weakness with areflexia
Forced Vital Capacity <20 mL/kg or rapidly declining
20-30-40 Rule: FVC <20 mL/kg OR MIP <30 cmH₂O OR MEP <40 cmH₂O → intubate
Bulbar dysfunction: dysphagia, dysarthria, aspiration risk
Autonomic instability: labile HR/BP, cardiac arrhythmias, syncope
Hughes score ≥4 (bedbound / ventilated)
Rapid progression: <5 days from onset to peak weakness
PATHOGENESIS
›
01
Trigger Infection
C. jejuni, CMV, EBV, SARS-CoV-2
Lipooligosaccharides on pathogen mimic gangliosides (GM1, GD1a, GQ1b) on peripheral nerves → molecular mimicry
›
02
Antibody Production
Anti-ganglioside IgG/IgM antibodies formed (anti-GM1, anti-GD1a, anti-GQ1b, anti-GD1b) target gangliosides on peripheral nerve axons/myelin
›
03
Complement Activation
Antibody binding → complement cascade → MAC (membrane attack complex) deposition → Schwann cell / axonal membrane injury
›
04
T-Cell Infiltration
T-lymphocytes & macrophages cross blood-nerve barrier → endoneurial infiltration → macrophage-mediated stripping of myelin or direct axonal attack
05
Nerve Dysfunction
Demyelination → slowed conduction, conduction block
Axonal injury → Wallerian degeneration → denervation, prolonged recovery
SUBTYPES & VARIANTS
AIDP
Acute Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy
Most common in western countries (~85%)
Demyelinating → slowed NCV, prolonged F-waves, conduction blocks
Anti-ganglioside: variable
EMG: demyelinating
AMAN
Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy
Common in Asia, children; assoc. C. jejuni
Pure motor; axonal attack; faster recovery than expected or severe
Anti-GM1, GD1a IgG
EMG: axonal motor
AMSAN
Acute Motor & Sensory Axonal Neuropathy
Severe; motor + sensory axonal loss
Slow/incomplete recovery; high disability rate
Anti-GM1, GD1a IgG
Poor prognosis
MFS
Miller Fisher Syndrome
Triad: ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, areflexia (no limb weakness typically)
May overlap with AIDP (Bickerstaff if encephalopathy added)
Anti-GQ1b IgG ≥85%
Good prognosis
BBE / PCB
Bickerstaff Brainstem Encephalitis / Pharyngeal-Cervical-Brachial
BBE: ophthalmoplegia + ataxia + encephalopathy
PCB: oropharyngeal + neck + arm weakness; bulbar predominant
Anti-GQ1b, GT1a
Bulbar risk!
CLINICAL FEATURES
Motor Features
Ascending symmetric flaccid weakness: legs → arms → bulbar → respiratory
Areflexia (or hyporeflexia) — hallmark finding, early and universal
Cranial nerve involvement: CN VII (bilateral facial palsy common), CN IX/X (bulbar)
Ophthalmoparesis: CN III/IV/VI (especially MFS/BBE)
Respiratory muscle failure: diaphragm + intercostals → type II respiratory failure
Children: may present with inability to walk, pain in legs, refusal to bear weight
Peak weakness: typically by day 14–28; plateau phase follows
Sensory & Pain Features
Paresthesias: glove-and-stocking distribution; often the first symptom
Neuropathic pain: back pain, limb pain (children often complain before weakness)
Vibration and proprioception loss predominates (large fiber dysfunction)
AMAN: sensory preserved despite severe motor involvement
Romberg sign may be positive (posterior column dysfunction)
Allodynia / hyperalgesia in acute phase
Pseudomotor signs: profuse sweating, especially at night
⚠ Autonomic Features
Sinus tachycardia (most common autonomic finding) or bradycardia
Labile blood pressure: hypertension alternating with hypotension
Cardiac arrhythmias: AV block, ventricular ectopy → continuous cardiac monitoring mandatory
Ileus, urinary retention, constipation
Hypersalivation, SIADH, orthostatic hypotension
Dysautonomia in 50–70% of hospitalized GBS; present in 25% of children
Avoid suctioning without premedication: vagal surges → cardiac arrest risk

NEUROLOGICAL EXAMINATION FINDINGS
Motor & Reflex Exam
AREFLEXIA / HYPOREFLEXIA
Deep tendon reflexes absent or markedly reduced globally; most sensitive early sign. Even in AMAN with preserved reflexes early — reassess serially.
FLACCID WEAKNESS
Hypotonia, no spasticity, no clonus. Proximal ≥ distal pattern typical. Symmetric bilaterally. Hip flexion often early (inability to stand from squat).
CRANIAL NERVE PALSY
Bilateral CN VII palsy in 50%; dysphagia/dysphonia (CN IX/X); extraocular palsy (MFS). Absent gag reflex → aspiration risk.
ABSENT: UMN SIGNS
No Babinski/Hoffman, no spasticity, no hyperreflexia → distinguishes from myelopathy/cord compression.
Sensory, Cranial & Special Tests
SENSORY LOSS (AIDP/AMSAN)
Vibration ↓↓ (125Hz tuning fork), proprioception ↓↓ (position sense), light touch and pinprick variably reduced. Glove-and-stocking pattern.
ROMBERG SIGN
Positive (falls with eyes closed) when proprioceptive loss prominent → posterior column / large fiber sensory neuropathy.
OPHTHALMOPLEGIA / PTOSIS
Full or partial; all directions affected (MFS). Pupillary light reflex usually spared. Diplopia = early MFS presentation.
RESPIRATORY ASSESSMENT
Paradoxical breathing (abdominal = diaphragm loss), count-to-20 in single breath, FVC at bedside. Neck flexor weakness predicts ventilator need.
DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP
Brighton Collaboration Diagnostic Criteria (Levels 1–4)
LEVEL 1 (Highest certainty)
Bilateral flaccid weakness limbs + DTR ↓/absent + monophasic + CSF: cytoalbuminous dissociation + EMG: peripheral neuropathy pattern
LEVEL 2
Bilateral flaccid weakness + DTR ↓/absent + monophasic + (CSF OR EMG criteria met, not both required)
LEVEL 3
Bilateral flaccid weakness + DTR ↓/absent + monophasic course only (no CSF/EMG data available)
LEVEL 4 (Lowest/Unclassified)
GBS suspected but does not meet levels 1–3, OR alternative dx confirmed

Additional Laboratory Investigations
SEROLOGY & ANTIBODIES
Anti-ganglioside panel: Anti-GM1, GD1a (AMAN), GQ1b (MFS/BBE), GT1a (PCB), GD1b
Anti-GQ1b positive in >85% of MFS cases
Campylobacter stool PCR/culture; serology for CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma, SARS-CoV-2
ROUTINE BLOODS
CBC, CMP, electrolytes (Na⁺ for SIADH), LFTs
Hyponatremia: monitor (SIADH in 10–15%)
CK mildly elevated (muscle disuse); ESR/CRP usually normal
Blood cultures if febrile + LP in children (exclude meningitis first)
RESPIRATORY MONITORING
Bedside spirometry q4h: FVC (target: ml/kg)
MIP (NIF) and MEP serial monitoring
ABG if FVC declining; SpO₂ continuous
Trend is more important than absolute value

TREATMENT
🚫
Corticosteroids — CONTRAINDICATED
Steroids do NOT improve outcomes in GBS and may worsen prognosis. Multiple RCTs negative. Do NOT give dexamethasone or methylprednisolone for GBS (different from CIDP).
⚠
Treatment Window
IVIg and PLEX are effective only within 2–4 weeks of onset. Combination of both not superior to either alone. Choose one modality.
Disease-Modifying Therapy
IVIg
First-line for children (preferred over PLEX for ease of administration)
Dose: 1 g/kg/day × 2 days OR 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days (equivalent efficacy)
Mechanism: Fc receptor blockade, complement inhibition, anti-idiotype antibodies
Contraindications: IgA deficiency (anaphylaxis risk — check IgA level before), severe heart failure
Side effects: headache, aseptic meningitis, thromboembolic events, hemolytic anemia, renal failure (sucrose-based)
Monitor: IgA level, renal function, CBC; use non-sucrose-based preparation in renal impairment
PLEX
Equivalent efficacy to IVIg; preferred if IVIg contraindicated or failed
Protocol: 4–6 sessions over 8–10 days; alternate days; 200–250 mL/kg total plasma volume removed
Replace with albumin (FFP if coagulopathy)
Pediatric challenges: vascular access (central line), hemodynamic instability in young children
Contraindications: active sepsis, severe coagulopathy, hemodynamic instability
Complications: hypotension, hypocalcemia, line infections, thrombosis
Re-treatment
Treatment-related fluctuation (TRF): 5–10% worsen after initial improvement → repeat IVIg course
Distinguish TRF from CIDP: TRF occurs within 8 weeks of treatment, CIDP by definition >8–12 weeks
Second dose IVIg (SID-GBS trial): may benefit patients with high IGOS score or low IVIg serum level after first dose
ICU Supportive Care
Airway
Early planned intubation preferred over emergent (20-30-40 Rule)
RSI: avoid succinylcholine (hyperkalemia risk); use rocuronium
NIV (BiPAP) may delay intubation in select patients; caution in bulbar dysfunction
Tracheostomy: consider if prolonged ventilation expected (>2 weeks)
Autonomic
Continuous cardiac monitoring (telemetry) mandatory
Labile BP: use short-acting agents (avoid long-acting); careful with antihypertensives → hypotension
Bradycardia: transcutaneous pacing available; atropine for acute severe bradycardia
Ileus: NGT feeds; bowel regimen; urinary catheter for retention
Avoid beta-blockers if labile BP; avoid anticholinergics
Pain / DVT
Neuropathic pain: gabapentin (10–35 mg/kg/day ÷ TID) or pregabalin (adolescents)
Opioids: cautious use (↑ ileus, sedation); IV morphine/oxycodone for severe pain
DVT prophylaxis: LMWH (enoxaparin) once ambulation lost; sequential compression devices
Consider: IV ketamine infusion for refractory neuropathic pain
Rehab / Nursing
Early physio/OT consultation; passive ROM from day 1
Pressure area care; heel padding; eye care if incomplete lid closure
Nutritional support: dietitian, NGT/NG feeds if dysphagia; NG/NJ if ileus
Psychological support: conscious ventilated patients require communication aids, anxiolytics
Hughes Functional Scale — Monitoring Tool
0
Healthy, no symptoms
1–2
Minor symptoms; able to run/walk unaided
3
Walks >5m with aid; ward-bound
4–6
Bedbound (4) → ventilated (5) → dead (6) → ICU LEVEL
Hughes ≥4 = indication for disease-modifying treatment. Score >5 = ICU-level care required.
PROGNOSIS
Outcome in Children
Walk independently at 6 months~80%

Mortality (ICU cohort)~3-5%
Poor Prognostic Factors (IGOS Score)
Rapid progression to peak weakness (<7 days)
Preceding C. jejuni infection (AMAN phenotype)
Axonal subtype (AMAN/AMSAN) on EMG
Mechanical ventilation required at presentation
Older age (paradoxically better in younger children)
Absent SNAPs and absent CMAPs on EMG
Hughes score ≥5 at nadir
Low IVIg serum level post-treatment (inadequate dosing)
Good factors: MFS subtype, young age, slow progression, sensory > motor
IGOS Score (International GBS Outcome Study)
Used to predict inability to walk independently at 6 months. Factors include age, preceding diarrhea, GBS disability score at admission, and mean distal CMAP amplitude. Score ≥6 predicts need for ventilatory support. Used clinically to guide treatment aggressiveness and ICU resource planning.
PICU CLINICAL PEARLS
Ventilator Management
Lung-protective ventilation: TV 6–8 mL/kg, PEEP 5–8 cmH₂O
Preserve spontaneous breathing if possible (CPAP/SIMV)
Avoid neuromuscular blockers — masks clinical neurological assessment
Weaning: SBT only when recovery evident; FVC trend upward ×48h
Aggressive weaning plan; most children extubate within 4–8 weeks
Autonomic Crisis Protocol
Severe hypertension: labetalol IV (avoid long-acting agents)
Hypotension: IV fluids first; vasopressors if needed
Bradycardia: transcutaneous pacing on standby
NO vigorous suctioning without atropine premedication
Avoid β-blockers (may worsen hypotensive episodes)
Careful opioid dosing (↑ vagal tone)
Monitoring Checklist
✓ FVC q4–6h (or more frequent if declining)
✓ Continuous cardiac telemetry
✓ Daily neurological exam (Hughes score)
✓ Peripheral nerve pain assessment (FLACC in young children)
✓ Nutritional assessment daily
✓ Pressure area inspection q shift
✓ DVT prophylaxis review
✓ Psychosocial needs family + patient
